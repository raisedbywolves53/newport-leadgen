## 3. LATAM Trade Regulatory Assessment

### Bottom Line Up Front

Newport can import candy from LATAM. There are no regulatory show-stoppers, but there are three compliance requirements that need attention **before** the first import:

1. **FSVP (Foreign Supplier Verification Program)** — mandatory FDA program, $10K-$20K setup
2. **Lead testing** — critical for Mexican and Central American candy, $50-$150/sample
3. **Color additive audit** — several colors common in LATAM are banned in the US

### Tariff Situation — Very Favorable

| Trade Agreement | Countries | Candy Tariff (2026) |
|---|---|---|
| USMCA | Mexico | **Free** on all finished confectionery |
| CAFTA-DR | Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras, Nicaragua | **Free or near-free** (phase-outs nearly complete by 2026) |
| Colombia TPA | Colombia | **Free** |
| No agreement | Brazil, Argentina | 5.6%-12.2% MFN rates |

**Key insight:** Finished candy products (HTS 1704, 1806) are generally NOT subject to US sugar tariff-rate quotas. The sugar TRQs target raw/refined sugar (HTS 1701), not manufactured confectionery. This is a common misconception — many potential importers assume sugar quotas block candy imports. They don't.

### Critical Compliance Requirements

**1. FSVP (Foreign Supplier Verification Program) — 21 CFR Part 1, Subpart L**
- Newport (as importer of record) must verify that each foreign supplier meets US food safety standards
- Required: hazard analysis per food type, supplier evaluation, on-site audits or testing, corrective action procedures, 2-year recordkeeping
- Need a "qualified individual" — send someone to FSPCA FSVP training course ($500-$800, 2-day)
- **Setup cost:** $10K-$20K (consultant + training + first supplier audits)
- **Ongoing cost:** $5K-$15K per supplier per year (audit frequency varies by risk)
- **Timeline:** Begin 3-6 months before first import

**2. Lead Contamination — THE #1 Risk for Mexican/Central American Candy**
- FDA action level: 0.1 ppm (100 ppb) lead for candy consumed by children
- Historical problem: chili powder, tamarind, manufacturing equipment as lead sources
- FDA Import Alert 28-05 maintains a detention list for specific manufacturers
- California Prop 65: even stricter standard (~0.5 micrograms/day MADL). Non-compliance = lawsuits with $2,500/day penalties
- **Action:** Require Certificates of Analysis (COAs) from accredited labs for every supplier. Budget $50-$150/sample for independent testing. Set internal max at 0.05 ppm (half FDA level) for margin of safety

**3. Color Additive Restrictions**
- Several colors used in LATAM candy are **banned in the US**:
  - Amaranth (Red 2/E123) — banned since 1976
  - Ponceau 4R (E124) — not approved
  - Azorubine/Carmoisine (E122) — not approved
  - Patent Blue V (E131) — not approved
  - Quinoline Yellow (E104) — not approved
- **Action:** Audit every product's ingredient list against FDA approved colors (21 CFR Parts 73, 74, 82) before importing. Products with unapproved colors will be detained and destroyed at Newport's expense

### US Labeling Requirements

Imported candy must have US-compliant labels including:
- **Nutrition Facts panel** (updated format per 21 CFR 101.9, including "Added Sugars")
- **Ingredient list** in English, descending by weight
- **Allergen declaration** (9 major allergens per FALCPA + FASTER Act, including sesame since 2023)
- **Net weight** in both metric and US customary units
- **Country of origin** marking
- **Name and address** of manufacturer, packer, or distributor

**Practical approach:** Import in bulk with original LATAM labels. Apply compliant US sticker labels at Newport's Florida warehouse before distribution. Cost: $500-$2,000 per SKU for initial label compliance review and design.

**Products that CANNOT be imported:**
- Candy with embedded non-nutritive objects (toys, trinkets) — this is why Kinder Surprise is banned (21 USC 342(d)(1))
- Candy with >0.5% alcohol by volume (requires TTB licensing)

### Miami Port Advantages

Newport's Florida location is ideal for LATAM candy imports:
- Miami International Airport (MIA) is the #1 US gateway for LATAM trade
- Port Miami is the closest US deepwater port to the Panama Canal
- Extensive cold chain infrastructure (critical for chocolate)
- Large customs broker community specializing in LATAM food imports
- FDA Florida District Office experienced with LATAM food entries
- Transit times: Dominican Republic 2-4 days, Central America 3-7 days, Mexico 5-10 days (ocean)
