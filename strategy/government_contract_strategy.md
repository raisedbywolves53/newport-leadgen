## 1. The Opportunity

### Total Addressable Market

The U.S. government is one of the largest food purchasers on the planet. Across federal, state, and local levels, annual food procurement spending exceeds **$9–13 billion** — and that figure understates reality because it excludes pass-through spending via food service management companies and group purchasing organizations.

**Federal spending alone (annual estimates):**

| Agency / Program | Annual Food Spend | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| USDA AMS Commodity Procurement | $3.5–4.5B | School lunch commodities, TEFAP, emergency food |
| DLA Troop Support (Subsistence) | $1.8–2.2B | Military dining, MREs, operational rations |
| Federal Bureau of Prisons (BOP) | $380–420M | 122 institutions, ~155,000 inmates |
| VA Medical Centers | $350–400M | 170+ medical centers, 1,000+ outpatient sites |
| DeCA (Commissaries) | $4.5–5.0B | 236 commissaries worldwide (resale, not procurement) |
| Indian Health Service (IHS) | $50–75M | Hospitals and clinics on tribal lands |
| GSA / Other Federal | $100–200M | Federal office buildings, events, emergency |

**State-level spending (annual estimates):**

| Category | Annual Food Spend | Notes |
|---|---|---|

| State Education Agencies | $2.0–3.0B | State-managed school meal programs |
| State Hospitals / Mental Health | $500M–1.0B | Psychiatric and long-term care facilities |

| Category | Annual Food Spend | Notes |
|---|---|---|

**Total addressable market: $35–50+ billion annually** across all government levels, including managed food service pass-through.

**Newport's realistic serviceable market (Florida + Southeast, wholesale distribution):** $500M–$2B annually in direct supply opportunities.

### Why This Matters for Newport

Government food procurement is not just large — it has structural advantages that make it one of the most attractive market segments in food distribution:

1. **Governments print money.** Federal and state agencies have appropriated budgets that get spent whether the economy is up or down. During the 2008 recession, government food spending actually *increased* while private sector food service contracted 15–20%.

2. **Governments overpay.** Government procurement processes prioritize compliance, reliability, and social objectives over pure cost optimization. Winning bidders typically earn 5–15% higher margins than equivalent private-sector contracts because evaluation criteria weight factors beyond price.

3. **Governments pay on time.** Net-30 payment terms are standard and enforced by the Prompt Payment Act (federal) and equivalent state laws. Late payments accrue interest penalties to the government. Compare this to private-sector food distribution where 45–90 day payment cycles and bad debt are common.

4. **Contracts are long-term.** Government food contracts typically run 1 base year + 2–4 option years, providing 3–5 years of predictable revenue. Many contracts include escalation clauses tied to BLS food price indices, protecting margins against inflation.

5. **Barriers to entry keep competition low.** Registration requirements (SAM.gov, state portals), compliance overhead (food safety certs, insurance, bonding), and proposal complexity deter many distributors. Fewer bidders = higher win rates for those who show up.

6. **Switching costs are high for incumbents.** Once a vendor is performing well, agencies strongly prefer to exercise option years rather than rebid. The "past performance" evaluation criterion structurally advantages incumbent vendors.

### Newport's Current Position

Newport Wholesalers ($25M–$100M revenue, 25+ years in business, Florida-based) is a broad-line food wholesale distributor with established supply chains, warehouse capacity, and delivery infrastructure. However, Newport has **zero government contract history** — no SAM.gov registration, no past performance record, no established relationships with procurement offices.

This is both the challenge and the opportunity. Newport needs to build its government business from scratch, but the playbook for doing so is well-established and the barriers are mostly bureaucratic rather than operational. Newport already has the core capability (sourcing, warehousing, delivering food at wholesale prices) — what's missing is the government-specific wrapper: registrations, certifications, relationships, and proposal experience.

## 2. How Government Food Procurement Actually Works

Government food procurement operates across three tiers — federal, state, and local — each with different procurement rules, decision makers, and contract structures. Understanding how money flows through each tier is essential for targeting the right opportunities.

### Federal Tier

The federal government spends $6–8 billion annually on direct food procurement through several major agencies:

**USDA Agricultural Marketing Service (AMS) — Commodity Procurement**
- **What they buy:** Bulk commodities for school lunch (NSLP), food banks (TEFAP), tribal programs, and disaster relief
- **How they buy:** Solicitations posted on AMS Commodity Purchasing website and SAM.gov. Vendors must be on the **Qualified Bidders List** — a pre-qualification process requiring financial documentation, food safety certifications, and production capacity proof
- **Scale:** $3.5–4.5 billion annually in direct commodity purchases
- **Newport angle:** Become an approved vendor on the Qualified Bidders List. AMS specifically seeks new vendors to increase competition and supply chain resilience. Florida location is strategic for hurricane and disaster food relief staging

**Defense Logistics Agency (DLA) Troop Support — Subsistence**
- **What they buy:** Fresh produce, meat, dairy, and dry goods for military dining facilities worldwide
- **How they buy:** **Prime Vendor (SPV) contracts** — DLA awards multi-year contracts to a small number of prime vendors (Sysco Government Services, US Foods, Performance Food Group) who then source from distributors. DLA does NOT buy directly from smaller distributors
- **Scale:** $1.8–2.2 billion annually
- **Newport angle:** Become a **supplier to a prime vendor**, not a prime vendor bidder. Contact Sysco Government Services and US Foods to become an approved sub-vendor for Florida military bases (MacDill AFB, Eglin AFB, NAS Pensacola, NAS Jacksonville, Patrick SFB, Homestead ARB)

**Defense Commissary Agency (DeCA)**
- **What they buy:** DeCA operates 236 commissaries (military grocery stores) — they don't procure food centrally but instead stock through manufacturer direct-store-delivery (DSD) and distributor partnerships
- **Scale:** $4.5–5.0 billion in annual resale
- **Newport angle:** Apply as an authorized supplier/distributor for Florida commissaries. DeCA prioritizes American-made products and competitive pricing

**Department of Veterans Affairs (VA)**
- **What they buy:** Food for 170+ VA medical centers, community living centers, and domiciliary programs
- **How they buy:** Combination of national contracts (via Federal Supply Schedule) and local purchase orders from individual VA medical centers
- **Scale:** $350–400 million annually
- **Newport angle:** VA medical centers in Florida (Bay Pines, Tampa, Miami, Gainesville, Orlando, Lake City, West Palm Beach) can make local purchases under simplified acquisition thresholds. Build relationships with Nutrition & Food Service (NFS) managers at individual facilities

**Federal Bureau of Prisons (BOP)**
- **What they buy:** Food for 122 institutions housing ~155,000 federal inmates
- **How they buy:** BOP central office (Washington, DC) sets national contracts for major commodities; individual institutions and regional offices procure specialty and local items via SAM.gov solicitations for purchases > $25,000, open market for < $25,000
- **Scale:** $380–420 million annual food budget ($7.50–$8.50 per inmate per day)
- **Organizational structure:**
  - Central Office → national commodity contracts
  - 6 Regional Offices → regional blanket purchase agreements
  - 122 Institutions → local purchase orders, specialty items
- **Newport angle:** Florida BOP institutions (FCI Coleman, FCI Marianna, FCI Miami, FCI Tallahassee, FPC Pensacola) procure local fresh produce, dairy, and bakery products. Start with local purchase orders (under micro-purchase threshold of $10,000) to build past performance

**General Services Administration (GSA)**
- **What they buy:** Food and catering for federal office buildings, training centers, events
- **How they buy:** GSA Schedule contracts (Multiple Award Schedule), spot buys
- **Newport angle:** Lower priority — fragmented, smaller orders. Consider GSA Schedule listing after establishing government track record

### State Tier

State governments collectively spend more on food than the federal government, with corrections and education being the dominant categories:

**State Departments of Corrections (DOCs)**
- **The largest single food buyer in most states.** State prisons house 1.25 million inmates across 1,500+ facilities. Food costs run $3.50–$12.00 per inmate per day depending on the state and model
- **Two operational models:**
  - **Self-operated** (CA, TX, NY, MI, WA, MA, CT, ME): The state runs its own kitchens and procures food directly. These states issue food supply contracts through state procurement portals
  - **Contracted** (most other states): The state contracts with a Food Service Management Company (FSMC) — Aramark, Trinity, Summit — who then procures food through their own supply chains. Newport would sell to the FSMC, not the state
- **Florida:** Florida DOC contracts with **Aramark Correctional Services** for inmate food service across 143 major facilities (~80,000 inmates). To sell into FL corrections, Newport needs to become an approved Aramark vendor OR wait for the state to rebid the FSMC contract
- **Scale:** $4.5–6.5 billion annually across all states

**State Education Agencies**
- **What they buy:** Commodities and supplies for state-managed school nutrition programs
- **How they buy:** State cooperative purchasing agreements, statewide contracts
- **Scale:** $2.0–3.0 billion at the state level (plus more at individual district level)

**State Hospitals and Mental Health Facilities**
- **What they buy:** Patient meals, dietary supplements, therapeutic diets
- **Scale:** $500M–1.0 billion annually
- **Newport angle:** Smaller, more accessible contracts. State hospital food service directors often have purchasing authority for fresh and local items

**State University Systems**
- **What they buy:** Dining hall food, catering, athletics
- **How they buy:** Mix of self-operated and contracted (Aramark, Sodexo, Chartwells). University procurement offices have significant autonomy
- **Scale:** $1.5–2.5 billion annually
- **Newport angle:** Florida state universities (UF, FSU, UCF, USF, FAU, FIU) — contact dining services directors

### Local Tier

Local government food procurement is the most fragmented but also the most accessible for a new entrant:

**School Districts**
- **The largest local food procurement category by far.** 100,000+ schools serve 30 million children daily through the National School Lunch Program (NSLP) and School Breakfast Program (SBP)
- **How they buy:** Individual school districts (or cooperatives of districts) issue solicitations for food supplies. Procurement is governed by USDA rules (7 CFR 210/220) plus state and local procurement regulations
- **Key contacts:** School Nutrition Directors, Food Service Directors, Procurement Specialists
- **Florida school districts** (major): Miami-Dade (350,000+ students), Broward (270,000+), Hillsborough (220,000+), Orange (210,000+), Palm Beach (195,000+), Duval (130,000+), Pinellas (100,000+), Polk (105,000+)
- **Scale:** $15–20 billion nationally; Florida alone represents $1.5–2.5 billion
- **Newport angle:** **This is the single best entry point.** Florida school districts are large, procurement is relatively straightforward, local preference applies, and Newport's wholesale pricing is competitive. Start with mid-size districts (Polk, Seminole, Volusia, Brevard, Lee) where competition from national distributors is lower

**County Jails**
- **Scale:** $2.0–3.0 billion annually (3,000+ jails, 750,000 daily population)
- **How they buy:** County sheriff's offices or corrections departments procure food directly or contract with FSMCs. Procurement follows county purchasing rules — typically simpler than state or federal
- **Newport angle:** Florida counties manage their own jails. Contact county purchasing departments and detention facility food service managers. Many Florida county jails use local food distributors rather than national FSMCs

**Public Hospitals and Senior Services**
- **Scale:** $2.0–3.5 billion combined
- **How they buy:** Hospital purchasing departments, Area Agency on Aging (AAA) contracts for Meals on Wheels
- **Newport angle:** Secondary priority. Consider after establishing school district and corrections track record

### The Middlemen — Who Stands Between Newport and Government End Users

Understanding the intermediary layer is critical because in many government food procurement scenarios, Newport won't sell directly to the agency — they'll sell through a middleman:

**Food Service Management Companies (FSMCs)**
The Big 3 FSMCs control an estimated 77.5% of the contracted government food service market:

| Company | Government Facilities | Approximate Market Share |
|---|---|---|
| Aramark | 450+ prisons, 2,200+ schools, 500+ healthcare | ~35% |
| Compass Group (Chartwells, Morrison, Eurest) | 3,000+ schools, 400+ healthcare, military | ~25% |
| Sodexo | 400+ healthcare, 200+ corrections, education | ~17.5% |
| Trinity Services Group | 400+ prisons in 32 states | ~8% (corrections-specific) |
| Summit Food Service | 350+ prisons | ~5% (corrections-specific) |
| Others (ABL, CFS, Nutrition Group) | Regional | ~9.5% |

When a state contracts with Aramark to manage prison food service, Aramark becomes the buyer. Newport needs to become an approved Aramark vendor to access that business. The same applies to Compass, Sodexo, and others.

**Group Purchasing Organizations (GPOs)**
GPOs aggregate purchasing volume across multiple institutions to negotiate better pricing. Key GPOs in government/institutional food:

| GPO | Members | Food Volume |
|---|---|---|
| Foodbuy (Compass subsidiary) | 9,000+ members | $35B+ |
| Vizient (formerly Novation/VHA) | 4,500+ hospitals | $130B+ (all categories) |
| Premier | 4,400+ hospitals | $70B+ (all categories) |
| HealthTrust Performance Group | 1,800+ hospitals | $62B+ |
| UNA (United Natural Foods) | Cooperatives, food co-ops | Niche |

Newport should pursue **Foodbuy** membership as the most relevant food-specific GPO. Foodbuy membership provides access to competitive bid opportunities from thousands of institutional buyers (schools, hospitals, corrections, corporate dining) — without needing a relationship with each individual institution.

**Prime Vendors (DLA/Military)**
For military food service, DLA awards multi-year "Subsistence Prime Vendor" (SPV) contracts to a small number of distributors who serve as the single point of delivery for military dining facilities in a geographic zone. Current prime vendors include:

- **Sysco Government Services** — largest share
- **US Foods Government Services**
- **Performance Food Group (PFG)**
- **Dole Fresh Vegetables** (produce-specific)

Newport cannot realistically become a DLA prime vendor (requires national-scale distribution, $100M+ in infrastructure). The play is to become a **sub-vendor/supplier** to whichever prime vendor holds the contract for Florida military installations.

### How Money Flows

Understanding the flow of funds clarifies where Newport can insert itself:

```
Congressional Appropriation
    → Agency Budget (USDA, DoD, VA, DOJ, etc.)
        → Procurement Office (contracting officers, purchasing agents)
            → Solicitation (IFB, RFP, RFQ, Sources Sought)
                → Evaluation & Award
                    → Contract / Purchase Order
                        → Delivery & Performance
                            → Payment (Net 30, Prompt Payment Act)
```

At each step, there are specific people who influence or decide. The strategy for Newport is to build relationships **before the solicitation** — with food service administrators who write the specifications, nutrition directors who set menu requirements, and contracting officers who evaluate bids. By the time a formal solicitation is published, the vendors who've been engaged in pre-solicitation activities have a significant advantage.

## 3. The Contract Award Process — Step by Step

### Types of Solicitations

Government solicitations come in several forms, each with different rules and competitive dynamics:

**Invitation for Bid (IFB) — Sealed Bidding**
- Used when requirements are clear and well-defined (e.g., "deliver 10,000 lbs of ground beef per month")
- Award goes to the **lowest price, technically acceptable** bidder
- No negotiation — you submit a sealed price and either win or lose
- Most common for commodity food purchases
- **Newport's approach:** Win on price. Newport's wholesale model should be price-competitive

**Request for Proposal (RFP) — Negotiated Procurement**
- Used when requirements are complex or the agency wants to evaluate multiple factors
- Award goes to the **best value** bidder (not necessarily lowest price)
- Agency evaluates technical capability, past performance, management approach, AND price
- Common for food service management contracts and large supply agreements
- **Newport's approach:** Compete on total value — price + service quality + reliability + local advantage

**Request for Quotation (RFQ)**
- Informal process for smaller purchases
- Agency requests price quotes from multiple vendors
- Common for purchases between micro-purchase threshold ($10K) and simplified acquisition threshold ($250K)
- **Newport's approach:** Respond quickly and with competitive pricing. Many vendors ignore RFQs, so just showing up is an advantage

**Sources Sought / Request for Information (RFI)**
- **Not a solicitation** — the agency is exploring the market before deciding how to procure
- Agency asks: "Who can provide this? What capabilities exist? What should we expect to pay?"
- **Critically important for Newport:** Responding to Sources Sought notices puts Newport on the agency's radar, influences the solicitation requirements, and positions Newport as a capable vendor before competition begins
- Always respond to relevant Sources Sought in food categories — even if you don't plan to bid the eventual contract

**Pre-Solicitation Notice**
- Advance notice that a solicitation is coming — gives vendors time to prepare
- Posted on SAM.gov (federal) or state procurement portals
- **Newport's approach:** Set up SAM.gov saved searches and state portal alerts to catch these early

### Evaluation Criteria — How Bids Are Scored

For food procurement contracts evaluated on best value (most RFPs), agencies typically weight evaluation factors as follows:

**Price / Cost (40–60% typical weight)**
- Evaluated as price per unit, total contract price, or total cost of ownership
- Agencies compare offered prices against the Independent Government Estimate (IGE)
- Prices significantly below the IGE may trigger a "price realism" review (agency worries you can't actually deliver at that price)
- **How to score well:** Competitive but not unrealistically low pricing. Provide clear cost breakdowns. Show understanding of delivery costs, fuel surcharges, and market fluctuations. Offer price escalation tied to BLS indices (shows sophistication)

**Technical Capability (20–30% typical weight)**
- Delivery capacity: fleet size, cold chain capability, delivery frequency, geographic coverage
- Product range: can you supply the full breadth of items specified?
- Infrastructure: warehouse capacity, food safety controls, temperature monitoring
- Staffing: qualified personnel, food safety training, delivery driver qualifications
- Technology: ordering systems, inventory management, electronic invoicing, EDI
- **How to score well:** Detailed technical narrative with specifics (not vague promises). Include photos of facilities, fleet details, certifications. Reference specific equipment models and capacities. Show your HACCP plan, SQF certification, or equivalent food safety credentials

**Past Performance (15–25% typical weight)**
- THE critical factor for government contracts. Agencies want evidence you can deliver.
- Evaluated through: contract references, past performance databases (CPARS/PPIRS for federal), client testimonials, performance metrics
- For new government vendors: private sector references count but carry less weight
- **How to score well (as a new government vendor):**
  - Provide strong private-sector references from large institutional clients (hotels, restaurants, hospitals)
  - Highlight any food distribution experience at scale
  - Show reliability metrics: on-time delivery rate, fill rate, customer retention
  - Acknowledge limited government experience but emphasize 25+ years of wholesale distribution expertise
  - **Start with small contracts to build a government track record** — this is essential

**Socioeconomic Factors (5–15% typical weight, or set-aside)**
- Small Business set-asides: Contracts reserved exclusively for small businesses (under NAICS size standard)
- Additional preferences: HUBZone, Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned (SDVOSB), Women-Owned (WOSB), 8(a)
- State/local preferences: In-state vendor scoring bonuses (typically 5–10% price preference)
- **How to score well:** Newport qualifies as a small business under NAICS 424410 ($200M size standard). Determine if Newport qualifies for any additional socioeconomic categories. Register all applicable certifications in SAM.gov and state portals

**Quality / Compliance (5–10% typical weight)**
- Food safety certifications: HACCP, SQF Level 2+, GFSI-benchmarked
- Regulatory compliance: FDA registration, state food handler permits, USDA inspection (if applicable)
- Insurance and bonding: Commercial general liability, product liability, workers' comp, vehicle insurance
- Traceability: Lot tracking, recall procedures, supplier verification
- **How to score well:** Get SQF Level 2 or equivalent GFSI certification if not already held. Maintain complete traceability documentation. Have documented recall procedures and crisis response plans

### Threshold Levels — What Applies to Each Purchase Size

**Micro-Purchase Threshold: $10,000**
- No competition required — agency can buy directly from any vendor
- Contracting officer or cardholder uses government purchase card (GPC)
- No solicitation, no evaluation — just a purchase order
- **Newport angle:** This is the easiest entry point. Build relationships with agency food service managers who can make direct micro-purchases. Deliver reliably. Build past performance one order at a time

**Simplified Acquisition Threshold: $250,000**
- Streamlined procurement process (fewer documentation requirements)
- Typically requires quotes from at least 3 vendors
- Can be set aside for small businesses
- **Newport angle:** Respond to every relevant RFQ in this range in Florida. These are the "sweet spot" contracts — large enough to be meaningful, small enough that national distributors often don't bother competing

**Full and Open Competition: Above $250,000**
- Formal solicitation required (IFB or RFP)
- Published on SAM.gov (federal) or state procurement portals
- Full evaluation process with evaluation board, scoring, and formal award decision
- Typically requires detailed proposals (technical volume, past performance volume, price volume)
- **Newport angle:** These take more effort but represent larger, longer-term opportunities. Target full-and-open competitions where Newport has local advantage, small business set-aside qualification, or competitive pricing

## 4. Contract Schedules & Timing

### Federal Fiscal Year: October 1 – September 30

The federal procurement cycle is highly seasonal and predictable:

| Period | Activity |
|---|---|
| **Oct – Dec** | New fiscal year begins. Agencies execute recently awarded contracts. Planning for next year's requirements begins at program level |
| **Jan – Mar** | Requirements development. Program offices define what they need. Market research (Sources Sought, RFIs) published. This is when agencies are most open to meeting potential vendors |
| **Apr – Jun** | Peak solicitation season. Most RFPs and IFBs for October-start contracts are published during this period. Proposal deadlines typically 30–60 days after publication |
| **Jul – Sep** | Evaluation and award period. Also "use it or lose it" — agencies spend remaining budget before fiscal year closes. September is the biggest month for government purchase card spending and micro-purchases |

**Critical insight:** The best time to engage with federal agencies is **January – March** when they're doing market research. Responding to Sources Sought notices and requesting meetings with food service administrators during this window positions Newport for the April–June solicitation wave.

### State Fiscal Years — Vary by State

Most states operate on a **July 1 – June 30** fiscal year, meaning:
- State solicitations typically publish January – April for July-start contracts
- Budget pressure spending occurs in May – June

**Key exception states:**
- New York: April 1 – March 31
- Texas: September 1 – August 31
- Alabama, Michigan: October 1 – September 30 (same as federal)

**Florida fiscal year: July 1 – June 30**
- FL state agency solicitations peak January – April
- FL school district solicitations for next school year publish February – May
- FL county budgets typically follow the same July 1 cycle

### Typical Contract Durations

| Contract Type | Typical Duration | Option Years |
|---|---|---|
| Federal food supply (BOP, VA) | 1 base year | + 2–4 option years |
| DLA Prime Vendor (military) | 3 base years | + 2 option years |
| USDA AMS commodity | 1 year (seasonal) | Rebid annually |
| State DOC food service (FSMC) | 3 base years | + 2–4 option years |
| State food supply contracts | 1–2 base years | + 1–3 option years |
| School district food supply | 1 base year | + 2–4 option years |
| County jail food supply | 1–3 base years | + 1–2 option years |

### When to Start Positioning

**12–18 months before contract expiry:** Begin engaging with the agency. Attend industry days, respond to RFIs, meet food service administrators. This is when the agency is evaluating whether to rebid or exercise options

**6–12 months before:** Formal solicitations may be published. Prepare proposal templates, line up references, ensure all certifications are current

**3–6 months before:** Proposals due. Evaluation period. Award decision

### How to Find Upcoming Bids

**Federal:**
- **SAM.gov** — Set up saved searches for NAICS codes 424410, 424420, 424430, 424440, 424490, 722310 in Florida and Southeast region. Get email alerts for new solicitations
- **USASpending.gov** — Search for expiring federal contracts to identify rebid opportunities 12+ months ahead
- **FPDS (Federal Procurement Data System)** — Historical contract data showing who won what and when it expires

**Florida State:**
- **MyFloridaMarketPlace (MFMP)** — Florida's state procurement portal. Register as a vendor and set up category alerts
- **Florida FACTS** — Florida Accountability Contract Tracking System for state contracts

**Florida Local:**
- **DemandStar** — Aggregates local government bids across Florida counties and school districts
- **BidNet** — Similar aggregator for local government solicitations
- **Individual county/school district procurement websites** — Larger districts (Miami-Dade, Broward, Hillsborough) have their own portals

**Our tools:**
- **Contract Scanner (expiring report):** `python scrapers/contract_scanner.py --report expiring --months-ahead 18` — identifies federal food contracts coming up for rebid
- **Contract Scanner (opportunities report):** `python scrapers/contract_scanner.py --report opportunities` — active solicitations in food categories

## 5. Competitive Landscape — Who Has the Stranglehold

Understanding who dominates each segment of government food procurement is essential for finding openings.

### The Big 3 Food Service Management Companies (FSMCs)

**Aramark Corporation**
- **Government footprint:** 450+ prisons across 35+ states, 2,200+ school districts, 500+ healthcare facilities, 45 national parks
- **Revenue:** $16.5 billion (2024)
- **Corrections dominance:** Aramark and Trinity together manage food service for approximately 65% of contracted state prisons
- **Florida:** Holds the Florida DOC statewide food service contract (143 facilities, ~80,000 inmates). Also manages food service at several FL universities (UCF, USF) and healthcare facilities
- **Vulnerabilities:** Recurring quality complaints and lawsuits (Michigan 2015, Ohio, Indiana — inmate hunger strikes, maggots in food reports). Some states have moved away from Aramark specifically due to performance issues. Florida's DOC contract will eventually come up for rebid

**Compass Group (Chartwells, Morrison Healthcare, Eurest)**
- **Government footprint:** 3,000+ school districts (via Chartwells), 400+ hospitals (via Morrison), corporate/government dining (via Eurest)
- **Revenue:** $42 billion parent company (world's largest food service company)
- **Owns Foodbuy:** Their GPO subsidiary ($35B+ in purchasing volume) — an alternative channel to access institutional buyers
- **Florida:** Chartwells holds food service contracts at several FL school districts and universities. Morrison Healthcare manages dining at hospital systems

**Sodexo**
- **Government footprint:** 400+ healthcare facilities, 200+ corrections facilities, education, military
- **Revenue:** $24 billion
- **Florida:** Active in FL healthcare and education markets. Holds contracts at some FL government facilities

### TKC Holdings — The Vertical Monopoly

TKC Holdings deserves special attention because they've built a vertically integrated corrections food empire:
- **Trinity Services Group:** Prison cafeteria management (400+ facilities in 32 states). Prepares and serves inmate meals
- **Keefe Group (Keefe Supply/Keefe Commissary Network):** Prison commissary operation (~50%+ of national commissary market). Sells snacks, drinks, and personal care items directly to inmates

Having the same parent company control both the cafeteria AND the commissary in a prison creates powerful lock-in. If an inmate isn't getting enough food at meals (Trinity), they spend more at the commissary (Keefe). TKC's combined corrections revenue is estimated at $2–3 billion annually.

**Newport's approach to TKC:** Don't compete head-on with Trinity on prison food service management. Instead, explore becoming an approved vendor to Trinity (supply them food) or target facilities where Trinity does NOT hold the contract.

### Prime Vendors — The Gatekeepers

For military and VA food service:

| Company | Government Role | Annual Government Revenue |
|---|---|---|
| Sysco Government Services | DLA prime vendor (largest share), VA contracts | $3–5B estimated |
| US Foods Government Services | DLA prime vendor, VA contracts | $2–3B estimated |
| Performance Food Group (PFG) | DLA prime vendor (regional), healthcare | $1–2B estimated |

These companies act as gatekeepers for military food distribution. Newport cannot bypass them for military contracts — the play is to become a **local supplier** to whichever prime vendor holds the Florida military SPV contract.

### Where They're Beatable

Despite the dominance of big players, there are clear openings:

## 6. The Fraud Crisis — Why the Timing Is Perfect

### The Scale of the Problem

Government food procurement is in the middle of the largest fraud crackdown in U.S. history. The numbers are staggering:

### How the Fraud Worked — The Patterns

The fraud cases share common patterns that expose what agencies now look for:

**Shell companies and pass-throughs:**
- Companies created on paper with no real operations, no real employees, no real warehouses

- Companies misrepresent ownership, size, revenue, or disadvantaged status to qualify for set-asides

- Affiliation concealment — large companies create subsidiaries that hide their actual size
- The SBA's January 2026 mass suspension of 1,091 firms (25% of the entire 8(a) program) shows how widespread this is

**Insider corruption:**
- Government employees steering contracts to specific vendors for kickbacks
- The $550M USAID case: a contracting officer ran a decade-long bribery scheme
- The $66M SNAP case: the USDA employee responsible for *detecting* fraud was the one enabling it

## 8. Decision Makers & Internal Champions

### Who Actually Decides

**Contracting Officers (CO)**
- Have the legal authority to obligate the government — they sign contracts
- Federal COs hold a warrant (specific dollar authority)
- They follow the rules but generally defer to technical evaluators on who's the "best" vendor
- **Where to find them:** SAM.gov contract award data, FPDS, agency procurement office directories
- **When to engage:** During formal procurement (proposal submission, negotiations). Don't cold-call COs to sell — they're process-oriented and will direct you to the solicitation

**Contracting Officer's Representatives (COR)**
- Assigned by the CO to monitor contract performance day-to-day
- Often the person who knows the most about what's working and what isn't with the current vendor
- **Where to find them:** Ask the CO, or find them via the food service operation at the facility
- **When to engage:** Post-award (if Newport wins) or pre-solicitation (to understand current vendor performance)

### Who Influences the Decision — Internal Champions

These are the people who don't sign the contract but have enormous influence over what gets purchased and from whom:

**Food Service Administrators / Food Service Directors**
- Run day-to-day food operations at federal prisons, VA hospitals, military dining facilities, state institutions
- **Why they matter:** They write the technical specifications that determine who can bid and who will score well. If the spec says "must deliver 3x/week within a 50-mile radius with 48-hour emergency capability," that spec was written by the food service administrator based on their operational needs
- **Where to find them:**
  - Apollo search (Segment C and D)
  - Agency staff directories (most federal agencies publish organizational charts)
  - LinkedIn (search "Food Service Director" + "BOP" or "VA" or specific facility names)
  - American Correctional Food Service Association (ACFSA) membership directory
- **How to engage:** Attend industry conferences (ACFSA, SNA, ALA). Offer product samples. Ask about their pain points with current vendors. Position Newport as a reliable, responsive local option

**School Nutrition Directors**
- Set menu requirements that drive purchasing specifications for school districts
- Oversee the school meal program including procurement, menu planning, and compliance with USDA nutrition standards
- **Why they matter:** They decide what products go on the approved list, what vendors get invited to bid, and what menus drive purchasing volumes
- **Where to find them:**
  - School Nutrition Association (SNA) state chapter membership
  - Florida School Nutrition Association (FSNA) conference attendees
  - Individual school district websites (food service / nutrition department)
  - Apollo search (Segment C — "School Nutrition Director" titles)
- **How to engage:** Attend FSNA conference. Offer product tastings. Emphasize local sourcing capabilities, delivery reliability, and competitive pricing. Offer to do a "comparison order" at no obligation

**Wardens / Facility Directors (Corrections)**
- Can request vendor changes if food service is causing operational problems (inmate grievances, hunger strikes, health issues)
- Don't control procurement directly but can escalate complaints that trigger contract review
- **Where to find them:** BOP website (each institution has published leadership), State DOC facility listings, LinkedIn
- **When to engage:** Generally don't approach wardens directly for food sales. Instead, if Newport hears about food service issues at a facility (through ACFSA, news reports, inmate advocacy groups), that's a signal the agency may be looking for alternatives

**End Users Who File Complaints**
- Inmate advocacy organizations that document food service problems
- Watchdog groups and media that cover prison food conditions
- State legislators who push for food service improvements
- **Why they matter:** Dissatisfaction creates procurement events. When complaints reach critical mass, agencies rebid contracts
- **How to track:** Prison Policy Initiative, The Appeal, state corrections oversight boards, legislative committee hearings

## 9. How Governments Make Selection — Positioning Playbook

### Before the Solicitation (12–18 Months Out)

This is where contracts are really won. By the time a formal solicitation is published, the most prepared vendors have already shaped the requirements and built relationships.

**Respond to Sources Sought and RFI Notices**
- When an agency publishes a Sources Sought notice for food supply services, respond even if you're not sure you'll bid
- A good response includes: company overview, relevant capabilities, past performance summary, product range, delivery capabilities, questions about the requirement
- This puts Newport in the agency's database and may influence how they structure the eventual solicitation

**Attend Industry Days and Vendor Outreach Events**
- Many agencies hold "Industry Day" events before major procurements where they brief potential vendors on upcoming requirements
- BOP, DLA, VA, and large school districts all hold these events
- Show up, ask intelligent questions, collect business cards, follow up

**Build Relationships with Food Service Administrators**
- Request meetings with food service directors at FL BOP institutions, FL VA medical centers, FL school districts
- Frame it as "introducing our capabilities" — not a sales pitch
- Bring product catalogs and samples
- Ask about their current pain points, delivery challenges, product gaps
- Leave a capability statement (see Section 9)

**Join Relevant Industry Associations**
- **American Correctional Food Service Association (ACFSA):** Annual conference, regional events, networking with corrections food service professionals. Membership ~$150/year
- **School Nutrition Association (SNA) / Florida School Nutrition Association (FSNA):** Annual conference (10,000+ attendees), state chapters, school food service networking. Membership ~$75/year
- **American Logistics Association (ALA):** Military/government logistics. Annual conference connects suppliers with military buyers. Membership ~$500/year
- **Florida Association of Counties:** Access to county purchasing officials across FL
- **Florida School Boards Association:** Access to school district procurement

**Register on Every Relevant Procurement Portal**
- SAM.gov (federal) — free, required for any federal contract
- MyFloridaMarketPlace (MFMP) — FL state procurement, $50/year
- DemandStar — FL local government bid aggregator
- BidNet — additional local government bids
- Individual county procurement portals (Miami-Dade, Broward, Hillsborough, etc.)

### During the Solicitation

**Submit Compliant Proposals**
- The #1 reason vendors lose is non-compliance — missing a requirement, incomplete form, late submission
- Read the solicitation cover to cover. Create a compliance matrix mapping every requirement to your response
- Answer every question. Provide every document. Meet every deadline

**Provide Strong Past Performance References**
- Even without government contract history, Newport has 25+ years of wholesale distribution
- Prepare reference letters from major private-sector clients documenting: on-time delivery rates, fill rates, years of relationship, responsiveness
- Frame private-sector experience as directly relevant: "25 years delivering [X] million pounds of food annually to [Y] institutional clients"

**Competitive Pricing with Transparent Breakdowns**
- Government evaluators appreciate transparent pricing: base product cost + delivery cost + fuel surcharge + handling = total unit price
- Offer market basket pricing (fixed prices on top-50 items for comparison, remaining items at percentage markup from published wholesale list)
- Include price escalation mechanism tied to BLS food CPI (shows sophistication and protects both parties)

### The 3 Things That Actually Win Contracts

After all the process and paperwork, government food contracts come down to three factors:

**1. Past Performance (Build This First)**
- Start with micro-purchases and small contracts to create a track record
- Deliver flawlessly on every early contract — government past performance databases (CPARS for federal) create a permanent record
- One poor rating can haunt a vendor for years; one excellent rating opens doors for years
- Target: Complete 5–10 small government contracts with excellent performance ratings before bidding large opportunities

**2. Price Competitiveness (Newport's Natural Advantage)**
- Newport's wholesale model is inherently price-competitive vs. broadline distributors who mark up for service
- For IFB (sealed bid) procurements, lowest price wins — period
- For RFP (best value) procurements, being within 5–10% of the lowest price while scoring well on other factors usually wins
- Don't leave money on the table — price to win, not price to maximize margin on individual contracts. Government business compounds: win one → build past performance → win more → negotiate better

**3. Responsiveness (Just Show Up)**
- A shocking number of potential vendors simply don't respond to solicitations, don't submit on time, or submit incomplete proposals
- Many government solicitations receive only 2–4 bids (compared to 20+ in private-sector food distribution)
- Just showing up with a compliant, competitive proposal puts Newport in the top half of bidders
- Set up a systematic process: monitor solicitations daily, evaluate fit within 48 hours, decide bid/no-bid, prepare and submit 1 week before deadline

Newport needs a **1–2 page capability statement** tailored for government procurement. This is the "business card" of government contracting — every agency expects to see one. It should include:

- Company name, logo, and tagline
- Core competencies (3–5 bullet points: wholesale food distribution, cold chain logistics, broad-line grocery supply, Florida/Southeast coverage, 25+ years experience)
- NAICS codes (424410, 424420, 424430, 424440, 424490, 722310)
- DUNS number / UEI (Unique Entity ID — obtained through SAM.gov registration)
- Cage Code (assigned after SAM.gov registration)
- Small business certifications (SB, and any others: HUBZone, WOSB, SDVOSB, 8(a))
- Past performance highlights (even if private-sector initially)
- Key differentiators (Florida-based, 25+ years, broad product range, flexible delivery)
- Contact information with a dedicated government sales POC
