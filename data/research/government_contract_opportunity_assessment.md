## 1. Florida School District Food Supply Contracts

### How These Contracts Work

Florida school districts procure food supply through Invitations to Bid (ITB), issued by each district's procurement department. Districts operate under Florida Statutes Chapter 287 and Florida Administrative Code Rule 6A-1.012. The process is decentralized — each of Florida's 67 county school districts runs its own procurement.

**Contract structure:**
- Typically 3-year contracts with optional renewal years (e.g., 3+2)
- Awarded via lowest responsive/responsible bidder (ITB process)
- Posted on VendorLink, DemandStar, or district-specific portals
- Intent to award posted for 72+ hours before final approval
- Districts can also "piggyback" on another district's competitively bid contract (with vendor consent)
- Large districts split food procurement into multiple ITBs by category: dry goods, frozen, dairy, produce, meat, etc.

**Bid cycle timing:**
- Most districts align with the school fiscal year (July 1 - June 30)
- ITBs for food supply typically issued February-May for the following school year
- Contracts often start July 1 or August 1
- Some districts use rolling procurement throughout the year

**Concrete example:** Broward County Schools ITB 26-051 (Food & Nutrition Services, Material Group 16500) was awarded at $8,302,584.26, running 12/1/2025 through 11/30/2028. This appears to be one category of food within their total food program — Broward likely has 5-10 separate food ITBs totaling $60-90M.

### What Newport Can Realistically Win

Newport is NOT going to displace Sysco or US Foods as a district's primary broadline distributor overnight. The realistic entry points are:

1. **Category-specific ITBs** (meat, frozen, dairy, produce, dry goods) — $2-10M each
2. **Smaller/mid-tier districts** (50,000-100,000 students) — total food budgets $15-35M
3. **Specialty or supplemental supply contracts** — $500K-3M

**Realistic Year 1 target:** Win 1-2 category ITBs at mid-size districts. Value: $2-8M total.
**Year 2-3:** Expand to 3-5 contracts across multiple districts. Value: $5-15M/year.

**Incumbent retention reality:** School districts are price-sensitive. If you are lowest responsive bid, you win. The barrier is not incumbency — it's logistics capability and competitive pricing. Districts MUST award to lowest responsive bidder under Florida law.

### Expiring Contracts to Watch (2026-2027)

The Broward ITB 26-051 just started (12/2025, expires 11/2028), so that one is locked up. To identify expiring contracts, Newport needs to:

- Register on VendorLink for bid notifications across all FL district categories
- Register on DemandStar (Broward, Miami-Dade use this)
- Check each target district's procurement page quarterly
- Most districts publish current contract lists with expiration dates

| County | Avg Daily Pop | Est. Annual Food Cost | Self-Operated? |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|----------------|
| Pinellas | ~3,100 | $2.5-3.5M | **Yes** — industrial kitchens at South & Central divisions |

| Broward | ~3,500 | $2.8-4.0M | Needs verification |

**Cost basis:** Florida daily food cost per inmate averages ~$2.32 (Prison Policy Institute data). At 365 days, that's ~$847/inmate/year. However, this figure may be low; actual food purchasing budgets including ingredients, supplies, and waste are typically $900-1,200/inmate/year.

### How Counties Procure Food Supply

- County jails procure food through the county purchasing department or sheriff's office procurement
- Posted on county procurement portals (e.g., Pinellas County procurement, Polk County purchasing)
- Subject to county procurement ordinances (different from state statute, but similar competitive bidding requirements)
- Some counties use cooperative purchasing agreements
- Contracts are typically annual with renewal options

### Realistic Opportunity for Newport

Self-operated jail kitchens need raw ingredients: canned goods, dry goods, frozen meats, fresh produce, dairy. This is exactly what a food wholesaler supplies.

**Year 1 target:** Win 1 county jail food supply contract (Pinellas or Polk). Value: $1-3M.
**Year 2-3:** Expand to 2-3 county jail contracts. Value: $3-8M/year.

**Key insight:** These are smaller contracts but have less competition than school districts. Fewer vendors actively pursue county jail food supply because margins are thin and volumes are modest. This is Newport's advantage — a regional wholesaler with cold chain can serve these better than national broadliners who may not prioritize them.

## 3. Federal Micro-Purchase Opportunities

### Current Thresholds (Effective October 1, 2025)

- **Micro-purchase threshold:** $15,000 (raised from $10,000)
- **Simplified Acquisition Threshold (SAT):** $350,000
- **No competition required** for micro-purchases — contracting officers can buy from any SAM-registered vendor
- **Simplified acquisitions ($15K-$350K):** require only informal quotes (typically 3+), posted on SAM.gov

### How Micro-Purchases Work in Food Categories

Federal micro-purchases for food are primarily made via the **Government Purchase Card (GPC)** — essentially a government credit card. The GPC program processes ~$30B annually across ~100M transactions government-wide.

**Critical limitation:** Food and beverage purchases via GPC have restrictions and prohibitions in many agencies. The primary federal food purchases that go through GPC/micro-purchase:
- Military dining facilities (smaller installations)
- VA medical center cafeteria supplemental purchases
- BOP institution supplemental/emergency food buys
- Federal office cafeterias and events
- USDA field offices

**Volume estimate for food-category micro-purchases:**
- Micro-purchases are NOT posted on SAM.gov (they are below the reporting threshold)
- They are invisible to outside vendors unless you have a direct relationship with the buying office
- There is no central database of micro-purchases
- You cannot "scan" for them — you must be known to the contracting officer

**Reality check:** Micro-purchases are NOT a viable volume play for an outside vendor. They work through established relationships where a CO already knows your company. They are a byproduct of doing business with an agency, not a lead generation channel.

### Simplified Acquisitions ($15K-$350K) — More Promising

These ARE posted on SAM.gov. In food categories (NAICS 4244xx), there are meaningful volumes:

**Estimated monthly volume on SAM.gov (food NAICS codes):**
- BOP quarterly subsistence solicitations: ~100-150 per quarter (4 per institution x ~120 institutions)
- VA supplemental food purchases: ~20-40/month
- Military installation food: ~50-100/month (but most go through DLA Prime Vendor)
- Other federal agencies: ~20-50/month

**Total estimated food-related SAM.gov postings:** 200-400+ per month nationwide, most in the $15K-$350K range.

**For Florida specifically:** ~15-30 per month, concentrated at BOP institutions, military bases (NAS Jacksonville, NAS Pensacola, MacDill AFB, Patrick SFB, Eglin AFB), and VA medical centers.

## 4. BOP Institution Food Supply

### How BOP Procures Food

Each federal prison institution issues **quarterly subsistence solicitations** for food supply. These are:

- Posted on SAM.gov as competitive solicitations
- Typically set aside for **Total Small Business** (NAICS 311999, SBA size standard 500 employees)
- Broken into categories: national menu (dry/canned goods), meat, dairy/eggs, fish, produce, bread/bakery
- Firm Fixed Price, FOB Destination
- Quarterly cycle: budget projections due 5th day of August, November, February, May
- Individual institution contracting officers manage the process
- Awards often split by line item (best price per item)

### Florida BOP Institutions

| Facility | Security | Population | Est. Quarterly Food Value |
|----------|----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| FCC Coleman (complex of 4 facilities) | Mixed | ~7,100 | $1.5-2.5M |
| FCI Miami | Low | ~900 | $200-350K |
| FDC Miami (detention center) | Admin | ~1,200 | $250-400K |
| FCI Marianna | Medium | ~1,100 | $250-400K |
| FCI Tallahassee | Low (female) | ~825 | $175-300K |
| FPC Pensacola | Minimum | ~350 | $75-150K |

**Estimation basis:** BOP allocates ~9% of budget to food/utilities. With ~$34,700/inmate/year total cost, food is roughly $3-5/inmate/day = $1,100-1,800/inmate/year. Quarterly value = annual food budget / 4.

### What This Means for Newport

**FCC Coleman is the big prize** — it's the largest federal prison complex in the nation with 7,100+ inmates. Quarterly food solicitations for Coleman alone could total $1.5-2.5M.

**Total Florida BOP food supply:** ~$10-16M/year across all institutions.

**Realistic Year 1 target:** Win 1-2 quarterly subsistence contracts at 1-2 institutions. Value: $200K-800K.
**Year 2-3:** Become a regular supplier to 2-3 institutions. Value: $1-3M/year.

**Key challenges:**
- Must be SAM-registered and certified small business
- Quarterly solicitations mean constant bidding (16+ bids/year just for Coleman)
- Delivery requirements are strict (specific days, specific loading dock procedures)
- Past performance matters for repeat wins
- Competition from local/regional food distributors who already serve these institutions

## 5. VA Medical Center Food Procurement

### How VISN 8 Procures Food

The VA uses a centralized **Subsistence Prime Vendor (SPV)** contract — a single national IDIQ contract:

- **Current SPV contractor:** US Foods, Inc.
- **Contract period:** 11/1/2023 - 10/31/2024, with 4 one-year options (potentially through 10/31/2028)
- **Total SPV annual sales:** ~$263M nationwide across 171 VA Medical Centers, 160 VCS operations, 135+ OGA facilities
- **Managed by:** VA National Acquisition Center in Hines, IL

### VISN 8 Facilities (Florida)

- James A. Haley Veterans' Hospital (Tampa)
- Bay Pines VA Healthcare System (St. Petersburg)
- Miami VA Healthcare System
- Orlando VA Medical Center
- West Palm Beach VA Medical Center
- North Florida/South Georgia Veterans Health System (Gainesville/Lake City)
- Malcom Randall VAMC (Gainesville)
- San Juan VA Medical Center (Puerto Rico)

### Reality for Newport

**The SPV contract is a near-impenetrable barrier.** US Foods holds the national prime vendor contract. Individual VA medical centers order through this contract. Newport cannot sell food directly to VA medical centers outside the SPV framework.

**Exceptions:**
- Fresh bread, fresh milk, and fresh produce are **excluded** from the SPV contract — local vendors can compete for these
- Supplemental purchases outside SPV may occur as micro-purchases
- When the SPV contract recompetes (potentially 2028), there may be opportunities to become a subcontractor

**Realistic Year 1-3 value:** $0-200K (limited to bread/milk/produce if aggressively pursued). Not a primary target.

## 6. USDA Commodity Programs

### USDA AMS Qualified Bidders List

**What it is:** USDA's Agricultural Marketing Service purchases ~$4.8 billion/year in domestically produced food through competitive bidding among approved vendors.

**How to get on the list:**
1. Register in SAM.gov
2. Submit qualification package to NewVendor@usda.gov
3. Provide company letter, customer references, financial documentation
4. Non-manufacturers must submit supplier letters from manufacturers willing to provide product meeting USDA specs
5. Once approved, receive access to WBSCM (Web-Based Supply Chain Management System) to bid on solicitations

**Concentration risk:** Top 25 vendors receive 45% of all USDA food spending. Tyson Foods alone gets $238M (5%). This is a market dominated by large manufacturers and national distributors.

**Realistic for Newport:** As a non-manufacturer/distributor, Newport would need manufacturer partnerships. Typical awards for mid-size qualified vendors: $500K-5M/year. Getting approved takes 3-6 months.

### LFPA (Local Food Purchase Assistance)

**Florida LFPA allocation:** $14.88M (initial) + $21.67M (LFPA Plus) = ~$36.5M total
**Administered by:** Florida Dept. of Agriculture & Consumer Services (FDACS)
**Structure:** FDACS runs competitive grants to food banks and nonprofits, who then purchase food from local vendors (within 400 miles)

**For Newport:** You don't apply for LFPA directly. You become a supplier to the organizations that receive LFPA grants (Feeding South Florida, Second Harvest, etc.). This is a relationship play, not a procurement play. Potential value: $500K-2M/year if positioned as a regional supplier to LFPA grant recipients.

### TEFAP (The Emergency Food Assistance Program)

**Administered by:** FDACS Division of Food, Nutrition and Wellness
**How it works:** USDA purchases commodities and ships to state warehouses. States distribute to food banks. Vendors deliver commodities to state-selected distribution points.

**For Newport:** Contact FDACS to become an approved TEFAP vendor/distributor. The opportunity is in logistics/distribution, not in selling food. Potential value: $200K-1M/year for distribution services.

## 7. Emergency/Disaster Food Contracts

### FEMA Advance Contracts

FEMA pre-positions contracts for food (MREs, shelf-stable meals), water, and other commodities BEFORE disasters strike. These are:

- Competed and awarded in advance of disaster declarations
- Multi-year IDIQ contracts with specific delivery requirements
- Awarded to vendors who can guarantee rapid deployment (24-72 hour delivery)
- Current advance contracts are held by large national logistics/food companies

**How to get positioned:**
1. Register in SAM.gov
2. Complete FEMA Industry Liaison Program (ILP) Vendor Profile
3. Submit to fema-industry@fema.dhs.gov
4. Monitor SAM.gov for FEMA solicitations

**Are these sole-source?** No. FEMA advance contracts are competitively awarded BEFORE disasters. During a disaster, FEMA can sole-source for immediate needs, but the preference is for pre-positioned contract vehicles.

### Florida Division of Emergency Management

**Contact:** Logistics Contracts and Vendor Specialist (850-815-4606)
**Registration:** Must register in MyFloridaMarketPlace (MFMP) + Vendor Information Portal
**What they need:** Pre-positioned food, water, and supply agreements for hurricane season

**For Newport:**
- Register in MFMP and as a FDEM vendor
- Position for regional food supply during hurricane response
- Florida's location makes this a real opportunity — FDEM needs regional vendors with warehouse capacity and truck fleets

**Realistic value:** Highly variable. In a no-hurricane year: $0. In an active hurricane season: $500K-5M+ in emergency food supply. Average over 3 years: $300K-1M/year.

**Key advantage for Newport:** FEMA and FDEM prefer local vendors for disaster response when practical. A Florida-based wholesaler with cold chain, trucks, and warehouse space is exactly what they need.

## 9. Contract Lifecycle Details

### Florida School Districts

- **Typical term:** 3 years, with 1-2 optional renewal years
- **Renewal pattern:** If performance is satisfactory and prices remain competitive, districts typically renew
- **Solicitation lead time:** ITBs posted 30-60 days before bid due date; 60-90 days before contract start
- **Start/end dates:** Most align with school year (July-June) or fiscal year; some start mid-year
- **Incumbent advantage:** Moderate. Florida law requires award to lowest responsive bidder, so price wins. But incumbents know the specifications better.

### County Jail Contracts

- **Typical term:** 1-3 years with renewal options
- **Renewal pattern:** Counties prefer to keep good vendors; rebid every 3-5 years
- **Solicitation lead time:** Posted 30-45 days before due date
- **Incumbent advantage:** Moderate to high. Jail food supply has switching costs (delivery schedules, product familiarity).

### BOP Quarterly Subsistence

- **Typical term:** 1 quarter (3 months)
- **Cycle:** Solicitations posted ~30-45 days before quarter start
- **Renewal pattern:** No renewal — each quarter is a new competition
- **Incumbent advantage:** Low to moderate. Price is king. Past performance helps but doesn't lock you in.
- **Quarterly calendar:**
  - Q1 (Oct-Dec): Solicited Aug-Sep
  - Q2 (Jan-Mar): Solicited Nov-Dec
  - Q3 (Apr-Jun): Solicited Feb-Mar
  - Q4 (Jul-Sep): Solicited May-Jun

### USDA AMS Commodity Procurement

- **Typical term:** Delivery orders under annual solicitations
- **Cycle:** Ongoing — AMS issues solicitations continuously
- **Incumbent advantage:** Low. Each solicitation is a new competition among qualified bidders.

## 10. Nationwide Micro-Purchase and Small Contract Volume

### Micro-Purchases (Under $15K)

**Scanning automation verdict: NOT VIABLE.**

- Micro-purchases are NOT posted on SAM.gov
- Made via Government Purchase Card (GPC) — essentially credit card swipes
- No public notification or posting requirement
- Volume exists (~$30B/year across all categories) but is invisible to outside vendors
- Food-specific GPC purchases have agency restrictions
- Only way to capture these: have existing relationships with buying offices

### Simplified Acquisitions ($15K-$350K) — Viable for Automation

**Estimated nationwide volume in food NAICS codes:**

| Source | Monthly Postings | Annual Postings | Annual Value |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|-------------|
| BOP Quarterly Subsistence | 100-150 | 400-600 | $200-600M |
| Military/DLA (non-SPV) | 50-100 | 600-1,200 | $100-300M |
| VA (non-SPV) | 20-40 | 240-480 | $50-150M |
| USDA/Other Federal | 30-60 | 360-720 | $50-200M |
| **Total** | **200-350** | **1,600-3,000** | **$400M-1.25B** |

**Is automated scanning viable?** Yes, for simplified acquisitions. SAM.gov has an API. The existing contract scanner tool (`scrapers/contract_scanner.py`) can be extended to:

1. Pull daily new postings in food NAICS codes
2. Filter by geography (FL for local delivery, nationwide for shipped)
3. Alert on new opportunities matching Newport's capabilities
4. Track response deadlines

**But there are practical limits:**
- Each bid requires preparation (reviewing specs, pricing, compliance docs)
- A realistic bid capacity: 3-5 simplified acquisition bids/week with dedicated staff
- Win rate on new-to-vendor opportunities: 10-15%
- Revenue per win: $15K-350K (average ~$75K)
- Expected annual wins from scanning: 20-40
- Expected annual revenue: $1.5-3M

**Nationwide simplified acquisition scanning is viable as a supplemental revenue stream, not a primary strategy.** It requires dedicated bid preparation resources and works best when focused on geographically proximate opportunities where Newport has delivery capability.
